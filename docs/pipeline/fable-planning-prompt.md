# Fable Planning Session: permits.ecbtx.com to A, Then Sell It

Run once in the Claude chat interface (free until June 22), not Claude Code. Paste this prompt, then below it paste the context pack: the full component grade table with evidence from the audit session, the git log excerpt showing branch state, and the route count and auth findings. Do not paste source code; the audit evidence already names the specifics. The session ends when PLAN.md is output in full. Save it to docs/pipeline/PLAN.md in the permit-api repo.

```prompt
You are the planning layer of a three tier pipeline: you plan once, an Opus orchestrator executes in Claude Code on subscription, Sonnet subagents do mechanical building, a CI gate and automated checks verify everything, and you only see this again as bounded evidence packets for the risky phases. No strategic decision may remain downstream. You are planning, not implementing.

Before producing PLAN.md, ask any clarifying questions whose answers would change the plan's structure, and wait for answers. The offer decision named below is one such input if the owner has not already supplied it. If nothing would change the structure, proceed directly.

The product: permits.ecbtx.com, a permit data API business. Current overall grade C+ with the component breakdown pasted below this prompt. The shape of the problem: the data layer is an A (631 scrapers, 3.18B rows catalogued, enrichment cascade gated at 98.16 percent), the business layer is an F ($0 revenue, zero paying customers), and operations sit in between (prod running off a long lived feature branch by manual merge and restart, a Railway backup that auto deploys a different branch than prod actually runs, a single R730 behind one cloudflared tunnel that has now survived two power outages while sharing the box with dual 3090s pulling 170W plus on enrichment, an untested T430 failover, a single Postgres primary with known OOM behavior, an unpinned curl pipe sh Tailscale install and unverified cloudflared binary in the Dockerfile).

One decision input the plan cannot proceed without: this asset is at least three distinct businesses wearing one domain. A self serve permit data API for developers, hail lead lists for roofers, and septic lead lists for septic contractors. They do not share a funnel, a price shape, or an email campaign. Which offer the campaign sells is an owner decision. The plan must name it as a required input by a named phase, with the analysis the orchestrator can prepare to inform it: funnel shape per offer, effort delta to campaign readiness per offer, and revenue math per offer. Plan the plumbing common to all three, and structure the campaign phase to branch on the owner's choice. Do not plan all three campaigns shallowly; depth goes to the common foundation and to the decision analysis.

Your mandate: a campaign plan that takes every component to an A, ending with email campaign readiness, because the owner intends to start email marketing once the grades justify putting traffic on the product.

One strategic truth you must build the plan around rather than hide: Monetization is graded F because nothing has shipped with a price on it, and that grade cannot reach A through engineering work alone. It moves when a paying customer exists. So the email campaign is not a reward that comes after the A; it is the final phase OF the plan, gated behind the ops, resilience, and security fixes that make it safe to invite strangers to depend on this API. Sequence accordingly: harden first, instrument the funnel, then ship the offer. Marketing a product hosted on a single residential box behind one tunnel would convert the resilience D plus into churned customers and refunds, so the gates are real, but waiting for perfection means the F never moves. Your plan must state explicitly which grades are launch blocking and which can be hardened in parallel with early customers.

Planning principles you must follow:

1. Grade per effort ordering, and the audit already told you the cheap wins: branch hygiene plus a CI gate takes Deploy/ops to B (get prod off the feature branch, reconcile what main should be, make Railway's auto deploy and the prod checkout the same code, clean the stray .bak and .premerge junk, then gate deploys behind tests); a UPS on the R730 plus pinned and checksummed Dockerfile binaries close the cheapest parts of Resilience and Security. Front load these.

2. Resilience to A is an architecture question; make the decision in the plan. Single residential R730 sharing power and thermals with a 170W enrichment workload, one tunnel, one Postgres primary, untested failover. Decide the target architecture explicitly: what moves to Railway or other hosting versus what stays on the R730 (the data moat and GPU enrichment stay home; the question is the serving layer and the database), what the failover story is (a tested T430 runbook at minimum, with an actual failover drill as the acceptance test), backup strategy with a restore drill not just a backup cron, and the UPS. Hardware purchases and hosting spend are owner approval gates with the monthly cost stated.

3. Security to A is a short list; enumerate it completely. Pinned and verified binaries in the Dockerfile, the /docs exposure decision made deliberately (public docs can be a sales asset for an API product; if they stay public, say so as a decision with rate limiting, if not, gate them), an auth review on every route in the OpenAPI spec confirming the 401 behavior verified in the audit holds across all 196 routes, secrets audit, and dependency pinning. Each item gets a verification condition.

4. Performance to A means load tested, not fast when idle. The audit explicitly flagged that no authed heavy query has been load tested. Plan the load test: realistic authed search, export, and bulk patterns at 10x and 100x expected early customer volume, against staging or off peak prod, with the Postgres OOM behavior specifically provoked and bounded (connection limits, statement timeouts, work_mem discipline) before customers can trigger it.

5. The product gaps are part of the A, not extras. The audit names them: the hail leads MV has no API and no refresh cron, and the septic leads have no phone numbers. Plan both: expose the hail leads through the API with a scheduled refresh, and plan the phone enrichment path for septic leads (the owner has local GPU capacity and existing enrichment pipelines; routing and method are yours to propose, cost per record if external services are needed goes to an owner gate). These two items are what make the email campaign have something concrete to sell.

6. Monetization plumbing gets finished and tested end to end. What the audit verified exists: signup, login, API key issuance and management routes, and 401 enforcement on data endpoints. What the audit did NOT verify: usage metering, tiering, and billing. Treat their state as unknown. The first monetization task is an investigation that inventories what actually exists, then the plan builds what is missing. The acceptance test is unchanged regardless of what the inventory finds: a fake customer taken through the entire journey, sign up, get a key, hit the API, exceed a tier, get metered, get billed against a Stripe test mode invoice. Pricing itself (tiers, numbers) is owner only; the plan defines the pricing decision as an input it needs by a named phase, with the analysis the orchestrator can prepare to inform it (competitor pricing scan, cost per query math from the load tests).

7. Email campaign readiness is the final phase with hard entry gates. Define the gates as verifiable conditions: deploy/ops at B or better with CI live, resilience drill passed, security list closed, load test passed at the volumes the campaign could plausibly drive, monetization journey green end to end, and the funnel instrumented (signup source attribution, API key activation tracking, conversion events). The campaign work itself: list strategy from the existing lead data, sequence drafts, deliverability setup (domain warmup, SPF/DKIM/DMARC verification on the sending domain), and a staged send plan starting with a small tranche. Campaign copy drafting is gated behind every non monetization component reaching A, confirmed by the re grade checkpoint; once that holds, copy drafting proceeds as Sonnet work with owner review. All actual sends and all pricing are owner button pushes. Nothing customer facing ships without the owner.

8. Per phase, produce: objective, full target list with specifics from the audit evidence, decisions already made, an investigation checklist for builders, routing (Sonnet for mechanical, Opus for architectural, owner for money and sends), dependencies, completion promise as verifiable conditions, an acceptance checklist a tester can verify from evidence alone, and the verification tier: CI plus orchestrator review by default, Fable evidence packets reserved for the risky few (the prod branch migration, the database and serving layer architecture change, the failover drill, the monetization journey, and the campaign go gate).

9. Milestones with grade math. Project the component grades after each phase, with checkpoints: after the cheap wins, after the architecture work, after load testing, at campaign readiness. Include a re grade checkpoint where the same rubric as the original audit is rerun and projected grades are validated against measured reality before the campaign gate opens.

10. Risk register and escalation. The three most likely failure modes (the R730 dies during early customer traffic, the prod branch migration breaks serving, a campaign send outruns capacity or deliverability) each with a tripwire. Escalation rules: orchestrator decides, builder retries, owner decides, or a new planning session with you. Standing constraints: pricing and all outward communications are owner only, hardware and hosting spend are owner gates with costs stated, credential work is owner only, and nothing renders or claims a number the system did not measure.

Output PLAN.md in full as a single markdown block: executive summary with the launch blocking versus parallel hardening split, the phase sequence in execution order, the offer decision section with the per offer analysis the orchestrator must prepare, the architecture decision section, the monetization journey, the campaign readiness gates and campaign phase, milestones with grade projections, deferred items with reasons, risk register, escalation rules. End the session after the plan. Do not begin any of the work and do not draft campaign copy in this session.
```
